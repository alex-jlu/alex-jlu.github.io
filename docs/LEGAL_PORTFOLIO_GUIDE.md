# Legal Portfolio Guide

**Disclaimer:** This document is for personal planning only. It is **not legal advice**. For anything high-stakes, consult an employment or media lawyer in your country. When in doubt, ask your former employer's HR or Department Head for written consent.

This guide covers what you can and cannot publish on your portfolio, based on general legal principles and specific employment contract language (Bosch and product development studio). Use it together with [CONTENT_GUIDE.md](./CONTENT_GUIDE.md) before setting `publish: true` on any case study.

---

## Quick summary: Bosch vs Agency

### Bosch (public consumer product on the market)

**You CAN:**

- Name Bosch as your employer and state your job title
- Name the **public, released product** you worked on
- Describe your role at a **high level** (methods, collaboration, general contributions)
- Use **your own photos** of the public product (showroom, retail, in-home) if they show only what any customer could see
- Link to Bosch's official product page instead of hosting their assets
- Use information that has **"become publicly available"** (Section 13 exception) — a mass-market product in people's homes qualifies
- Use clearly labeled **illustrative mocks** that are not presented as actual deliverables

**You CANNOT (or should avoid):**

- Share unreleased products, prototypes, lab photos, or internal UI
- Publish internal documents, CAD, decks, screenshots, or exports from work systems
- Copy Bosch marketing images from their website and re-host them without permission (copyright + Section 15/16)
- Take copies of company Material without **written Department Head consent** (Section 15)
- Share trade secrets, technical know-how, roadmaps, metrics, supplier info, or customer data
- Claim sole ownership or sole authorship of Bosch-owned work (Section 16)
- Present non-project images as if they were the actual delivered work

**Safest visual approach:** your own photo of the public product, or a link to Bosch's official page.

### Product development studio / agency (bankrupt employer)

**You CAN:**

- Name the studio as your employer and state your job title
- Use your **professional experiential knowledge** (*berufliches Erfahrungswissen*) after employment — written from memory, in your own words
- Write **anonymous case studies** by sector (e.g. "B2B SaaS", "Consumer IoT") without naming the client
- Describe general methods, collaboration, and your role at a high level
- Use clearly labeled **illustrative / recreated mocks** that are not presented as actual client deliverables
- Reference information that is no longer a protected trade secret under **§ 5 GeschGehG**

**You CANNOT (or should avoid):**

- Republish old agency case studies, screenshots, or deliverables — even if they were once on the studio website
- Copy content from Wayback Machine or saved project files
- Use **client logos** without explicit written permission
- Disclose confidential information to third parties (your portfolio) **without employer consent** (§ 8) — this is stricter than Bosch
- Refer back to **physical or electronic records** containing confidential info when building case studies (§ 8)
- Publish client project details, specs, repos, algorithms, pricing, or supplier/customer data (§ 8 Abs. 3)
- Present **Arbeitsergebnisse** (work results) as yours — all project outputs belong to the agency (§ 12)

**Naming clients on your public site:** **Amber / risky.** The agency website listing clients does not automatically give you personal permission to disclose to third parties forever. Safer: name sectors, not clients. If you name clients, keep it factual CV-style text only — no logos, no case assets.

**Safest approach:** anonymous, memory-based case studies + illustrative visuals. Do not reuse old studio files.

## The four legal layers

Every portfolio item runs through these, in order:

| Layer | What it means |
|---|---|
| **1. Your contract / NDA** | Often the strictest rule; can survive after you leave, and sometimes after the company goes bankrupt |
| **2. IP ownership** | The work usually belongs to the employer or client, not you |
| **3. Copyright / trademark** | Logos, product photos, UI, and brand assets are protected even if "public" |
| **4. Misrepresentation** | Presenting something in a way that could mislead a reader (German *UWG* / unfair competition law may apply) |

**Important distinctions:**

- **Public on the internet ≠ you have the right to repost it.**
- **Company bankrupt ≠ NDAs and IP rules automatically disappear.**
- **Your own photo ≠ automatically safe** — it depends on what the photo shows and what your contract says.

## General rules for any employer

### What is usually fine

- Naming the employer on your About page or CV
- Stating your job title and dates of employment
- Describing your role at a **high level** (methods, collaboration, general contributions)
- Referencing **publicly launched** products by name
- Using visuals that show only what is already in official public marketing

### What is usually risky

- Unreleased features, internal dashboards, prototypes, lab photos
- Internal metrics, roadmaps, research reports, customer data
- Client or partner logos without explicit permission
- Copying case study assets from a former employer's website (even if it was public once)
- Claiming sole ownership or sole authorship of employer-owned work
- Implying current endorsement by a client or employer

### Third-party assets

- Use fonts, icons, and stock imagery only when licensed for public web use
- Do not grab images from Google Images without verifying the license
- Prefer: Google Fonts, MIT-licensed icon sets (Lucide, Heroicons), properly licensed stock

### Site-level legal notes (Germany / EU)

- **Impressum:** Required for publicly accessible websites in Germany (TMG § 5) — name, address, contact email
- **GDPR:** A static portfolio with no analytics and no contact form has minimal GDPR surface area; add a privacy policy if you later add tracking or forms

## Scenario: Startup (products brought to market)

### Usually okay if the product is public

- Naming the startup and product
- Your role and high-level contribution
- Photos of the **released public product**
- Screenshots from public app store listings, public website, or marketing materials
- Process and collaboration at a general level

### Still check

- NDA and IP assignment agreement
- Investor/board confidentiality
- Unreleased features, internal dashboards, private beta UI
- Internal metrics, cap table, revenue figures
- Photos taken before public launch

**Rule of thumb:** If it shipped publicly and the visual matches official marketing, you are usually in safer territory. Pre-release or internal material is not.

## Representative / illustrative images

Using images that are **not** from the actual project is allowed **only if you do not mislead**.

### Risky

- Stock photo or unrelated UI presented as "my project"
- Recreated mock that looks like a real client product without clarification
- Before/after images not from the actual work

### Safe

- Clearly labeled captions:
  - "Illustrative representation"
  - "Recreated mock for portfolio purposes"
  - "Process diagram — not actual client UI"
- Abstract diagrams, wireframes, blurred/redacted compositions
- Figma reconstructions that do not copy identifiable client branding

Under German law, misleading professional self-presentation can fall under unfair commercial practices (*UWG*) if it could influence hiring decisions. **If the image is not from the project, say so directly under it.**

| **§ 12 Abs. 3 — modification** | Der Arbeitgeber hat insbesondere auch das Recht, die Tätigkeitsergebnisse … zu ändern bzw. umzugestalten | The employer may also modify/remake copyright-protected works while preserving their essential character | Even adapted versions belong to the employer. |
| **§ 12 Abs. 4 — transfer to third parties** | Der Arbeitgeber ist zur Weiterübertragung der vorstehend übertragenen oder eingeräumten Rechte an Dritte … befugt | The employer may transfer or grant usage rights to third parties if this serves the business | Client/agency may control project materials. |
| **§ 12 Abs. 5 — compensation** | Die vorstehende Übertragung und Einräumung von Rechten ist durch die Vergütung nach § 3 Abs. (1) abgegolten. | Transfer/grant of rights is compensated by salary under § 3(1). | You were paid for assigning rights — you don't retain publication rights. |
| **§ 12 Abs. 6 — surrender** | Auf Verlangen des Arbeitgebers hat die beschäftigte Person Arbeitsergebnisse … unverzüglich an den Arbeitgeber herauszugeben. | On employer demand, the employee must immediately surrender work results to which the employer has rights. | Reinforces: don't keep/use saved work files for portfolio. |
| **§ 12 Abs. 6 — inspection / no retention** | Der Arbeitgeber hat jederzeit das Recht, diese Arbeitsergebnisse einzusehen. Zurückbehaltungsrechte stehen der beschäftigten Person insoweit nicht zu. | The employer may inspect work results at any time; the employee has no retention rights | No legal basis to withhold or reuse deliverables. |

## How the clauses interact

| What you want to publish | Main clauses involved | Strict portfolio effect |
|---|---|---|
| "Design Engineer at Bosch" | None strongly against | **Allowed** |
| "Worked on [public product name]" | 13.2 product lines; 13.1 public exception | **Allowed** if product is publicly released |
| High-level description of your role | 13.1.2, 13.2 trade secrets | **Allowed** if general and non-technical |
| Your photo of the public product in a showroom/home | 13.1 exception, 15.1, 16.1 | **Generally allowed** if it shows only the public product and is your own photo, not a copied company file |
| Interior/cutaway image already on Bosch's public website | 13.1 exception, 16.1, copyright | **Confidentiality:** likely okay to show that public view. **Best practice:** use your own photo or link out; avoid downloading/re-hosting Bosch's file without permission |
| Downloaded Bosch marketing image uploaded to your site | 15.1, 16.1, copyright | **Caution** — even if public, it may still be Bosch-owned Material/a copy |
| Internal screenshots, CAD, prototypes, lab photos | 13.1, 13.2, 15.1, 16.1 | **Do not publish** |
| "Design Engineer at [Agency]" | None strongly against | **Allowed** |
| Anonymous agency case study from memory | § 8 berufliches Erfahrungswissen | **Allowed** — best approach for agency work |
| "Worked with [Client X]" on public site | § 8 Abs. 1 disclosure without consent | **Amber / risky** — safer to use sectors only |
| Client logos | § 8, trademark law | **Do not publish** without permission |
| Old agency case screenshots/text | § 8, § 12 Arbeitsergebnisse | **Do not publish** |
| Illustrative mock not from the project, clearly labeled | Misrepresentation risk | **Allowed** if clearly labeled as illustrative/recreated |

### Key clauses to remember

**Bosch — Section 13 public exception:**

> Information **"has become publicly available"** / **"öffentlich zugänglich geworden ist"**

**Bosch — Section 15.1 + 16.1:**

> No copies without Department Head consent; no use/disclosure of Material after employment

**Agency — § 8 berufliches Erfahrungswissen:**

> Use professional experiential knowledge after employment, **without referring to confidential physical or electronic records**

**Agency — § 8 Abs. 1 + § 12:**

> No disclosure to third parties without employer consent; all Arbeitsergebnisse belong to the employer

## Traffic-light summary

### Green — generally okay

- Employer name (Bosch or agency)
- Public product name (Bosch)
- General role description
- Your own photo of the public released Bosch product
- Link to Bosch product page
- Anonymous agency case study (sector/industry only)
- Memory-based agency case study (no old files)
- Clearly labeled illustrative mock

### Amber — okay with care

- Your own Bosch photo (confirm it shows no non-public detail)
- Public interior/cutaway view if identical to Bosch marketing
- Naming agency clients in text only (not logos, not case assets)
- Startup public app screenshots from app store / public website

### Red — avoid

- Internal documents, screenshots, CAD, lab photos
- Prototypes or unreleased versions
- Internal metrics, research, roadmaps
- Copied Bosch or agency files without written consent
- Client logos without permission
- Republishing old agency case pages (even from Wayback Machine)
- Claiming ownership of employer/client IP
- Non-project images presented as actual deliverables without labeling

## Pre-publish decision checklist

Before publishing anything on your portfolio, ask:

1. **Contract:** Does my employment/NDA allow this?
2. **Ownership:** Do I own this asset or description, or was it assigned to the employer/client?
3. **Public release:** Is this already in official public marketing / app store / press release?
4. **Identification:** Am I revealing client, unreleased product, or confidential process?
5. **Brand use:** Am I using a logo/trademark without permission?
6. **Truthfulness:** Could a reasonable reader think this image is the actual delivered work when it is not?
7. **Bosch-specific:** Does this fall under the Section 13 "publicly available" exception?
8. **Bosch-specific:** Am I copying company Material without Department Head consent (Section 15)?
9. **Agency-specific:** Am I using berufliches Erfahrungswissen without referring to old confidential records (§ 8)?
10. **Agency-specific:** Am I republishing Arbeitsergebnisse owned by the agency (§ 12)?

If any answer is uncertain, default to **no** or use an anonymous, recreated, clearly labeled version.

Keep a **private approval log** (email/ticket references) for anything borderline — not in git, just for your records.
